> [!axiom] Axiom (Linearity in the "dilution" of the experiment).
> $C(\alpha\cdot\sigma)=\alpha\cdot C(\sigma)$ for each signal $\sigma$ and weight $\alpha \in[0,1]$. 

^axm-pst-signal-linear-in-dilution


The axiom states that the cost of a signal is linear in the probability that it generates information, where $\alpha\cdot\sigma$ is the [[Signal Dilution]]. 

The axiom is applied to [[Prior-Independent Information Costs]] which measure the cost of obtaining the signal regardless of the agent's prior belief. 

If we view $C(\sigma)$ as the physical cost or _market price_ of the experiment $\sigma$, and the diluted signal $\sigma_{\alpha}$ is generated by running $\sigma$ with probability $\alpha$ (incurring the cost $C(\sigma)$ only then), then the expected cost incurred by acquiring $\sigma_{\alpha}$ should be $\alpha$ times the cost of the full signal $C(\sigma)$. 

This proportionality reflects the fact that costs scale linearly with the likelihood of actually receiving the informative output. Of course, by [[Axiom_1004ba21-f22d_ Consistency with the Blackwell Order|Blackwell Consistency]], the less informative mixture (with $\alpha \in[0,1]$) necessitates a diluted cost, less than or equal to the cost under the original signal, with equality holding only for $\alpha=1$. 

The linear structure is consistent with the broader class of [[Posterior Separable Cost Functions]], $C(p,\tau)=\mathbb{E}_{q\sim \tau}[\Phi_{p}(q)]$. To see that PS cost functions satisfy the Axiom, observe that the distribution over posterior beliefs given the diluted signal $\alpha\cdot\sigma$, $\tau_{\alpha\cdot\sigma}$, is the convex combination that puts weight $\alpha$ on the distribution $\tau_{\sigma}$ generated by $\sigma$, and weight $1-\alpha$ on the prior. 

So, 
$$
\begin{aligned}
C(p,\tau_{\alpha\cdot\sigma}) & =\mathbb{E}_{q\sim \alpha \tau_{\sigma}+(1-\alpha)\delta_{p}}[\Phi_{p}(q)] \\
 & = \alpha \mathbb{E}_{q\sim \tau_{\sigma}}[\Phi_{p}(q)] + (1-\alpha)\Phi_{p}(p) \\
 & = \alpha\cdot C(p,\tau_{\sigma})
\end{aligned}
$$
where the second equality uses the fact that $C$ is [[Affine Function|affine]] in $\tau$ and the third using $\Phi_{p}(p)=0$ by definition of PS cost functions.
