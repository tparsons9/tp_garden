**Berk Nash Equilibrium** is an equilibrium concept designed for strategic environments where agents might be [[Model Uncertainty and Misspecification under Bayesian Learning#Model Misspecification|misspecified]]–their subjective model of the world rules out the true signal-generating distribution.

>[!tldr]
>A strategy profile $\sigma$ is a **Berk Nash Equilibrium** if every player's strategy $\sigma_{i}$ is optimal given a belief $\mu_{i}$ that minimizes the KL-divergence between their subjective model and the objective consequences generated by the strategy profile $\sigma$ itself.

It can be defined within a [[Berk-Nash Equilibrium - Single Agent|single agent setting (Berk-NE)]] or to [[Berk-Nash Equilibrium - Simultaneous Games|simultaneous-move games (Berk-NE)]], where the objective distribution of consequences for each player depends both on the state $\Omega$ *and also* on the strategy profile chosen by all players. See those notes for formal definitions: 

- [[Berk-Nash Equilibrium - Single Agent]]
- [[Berk-Nash Equilibrium - Simultaneous Games]]

## Discussion


Standard Nash equilibrium relies on players best-responding to beliefs that are both correct and common knowledge. When an agent is misspecified, they do not know the true relationship (the objective mapping $Q$) between their actions, observed signals, and the consequences. Instead, they rely on a subjective model, or the parameterized family of distributions $Q_{\theta}$.

### Internal Consistency

In a Berk-NE a player's strategy must be *internally consistent* with their misspecified model. This consistency is achieved through conditions $(1)$ and $(2)$ of the definitions.

#### (1) Finding the Best-Fitting Model Parameter (KL Minimization)

Given a player's strategy $\sigma$ (which maps signals to actions), that strategy induces an overall distribution $q_{\sigma}$ over signal action pairs. This strategy, interacting with the true environment, generates objective distributions over consequences $Q(Y\mid s,a)$. The player must determine which parameter $\theta$ in their subjective model best explains the consequences generated by their own actions.

The closeness between the subjective model parameter and the objective reality is measured using the expected [[= Kullback-Leibler Divergence for Relative Entropy|KL-Divergence]]. The set of closest parameters, $\Theta ^{\ast}(\sigma)$, are those that minimize this expected KL-divergence. By [[Berk's Consistency Result]], there is convergence to the best-fitting model and the agent exhibits externally consistent behavior. For related topics, see [[Misspecified Beliefs Converge to the KL-Minimizing Parameter]] .

#### (2) Optimality

The chosen strategy must be a **best response** to the distribution of consequences derived from these best fitting beliefs.

### Existence 

> [!proposition] Proposition (Esponda and Pouzo (2016)).
> A Berk-Nash equilibrium exists. 

That is, Berk-NE is guaranteed to exist.
