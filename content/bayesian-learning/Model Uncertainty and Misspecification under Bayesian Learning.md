Under [[Bayesian Learning]], agents show disagreement between individuals, but they will eventually hold similar beliefs. It isn't clear how agents come to know $(P_{\theta})_{\theta \in\Theta}$. The assumption that agents perceive only one [[Signal-induced Belief Distribution]] $(P_{\theta})_{\theta \in\Theta}$ as possible means that agents never abandon their model, even as evidence accumulates against it (see [[Dogmatism as a critique of Bayesian Learning]])

Here we discuss two topics:
- *Model uncertainty*: Agents hold non-degenerate beliefs over the signal generating distribution
- *Model misspecification* : Agents assign probability zero to the true parameter.

## Model Uncertainty

We can introduce *model uncertainty* into the [[Bayesian Learning]] model by expanding the state space to $\Omega=\Theta \times\Gamma \times \mathcal{X}^{\infty}$, where the new parameter $\gamma$ indexes the signal-generating distribution, and the parameters $\theta$ and $\gamma$ jointly determine a family $(P_{\theta,\gamma})_{\theta \in\Theta,\ \gamma \in\Gamma}$ of the conditional distributions over signals. Note that, only $\theta$ is **payoff-relevant**. Use $P^{i}$ to denote the $i^{\text{th}}$ agent's subjective prior belief on $\Omega$, which is [[Common Knowledge]] to all agents. 

Under the standard model of [[Asymptotic Agreement of Beliefs]], agent's beliefs converge to each other. But whenever some small uncertainty is introduced, [[Failure of Asymptotic Agreement in Learning|asymptotic agreement can fail]]. 

## Model Misspecification

The misspecified learning model is one that the agent's set of prior beliefs assigns zero probability to the true data-generating process. That is, the agent is not simply uncertain about the signal-generating distribution, but in fact rules out the true distribution. 

The agent's beliefs will asymptotically converge to the parameter (within their subjective model) that is "closest" to the true distribution, ie., [[Misspecified Beliefs Converge to the KL-Minimizing Parameter]]. In misspecified environments, [[Berk's Consistency Result]] governs learning, ensuring that agents' beliefs will eventually concentrate on the parameter in the support that minimizes the KL-divergence. It essentially requires agent's to select the model that minimizes the informational distance from reality. This is distinct from [[Doob's Consistency Theorem]], which stated convergence results to the true parameter, whereas here we state convergence to the best-fitting model. 

Finally, [[Berk-Nash Equilibrium]] is an equilibrium concept designed for these misspecified environments, requiring that every agent's strategy is optimal given a belief that minimizes the KL-divergence between their subjective model and the objective consequences generated by the strategy profile itself. See [[Berk-Nash Equilibrium - Single Agent]] and [[Berk-Nash Equilibrium - Simultaneous Games]] for two definitions under different settings.
